The Headwaters Contract
Who Holds the Valve?
Ticon Recon Field Office · Craig

The Yampa River dropped below 120 cubic feet per second on April 21. That number triggers the city's first-ever codified water restriction ordinance — set for introduction April 28, 2026.

Ticon Recon identified seven undisclosed water right transfers tied to parcels adjacent to the closing Craig Station coal plant. The buyers: LLCs registered to a post office box in Ogden, Utah — same city as the parent company of the Craig Daily Press.

"The water isn't leaving Moffat County.
The control of the water is."

The augmentation plan transferred water rights from Tri-State to a newly formed trust. The beneficiary list remains sealed — accessible only to signatories of what insiders call "The Headwaters Contract." Next meeting: May 15, Elks Lodge, Steamboat Springs.

The Integrity of the Grid

Craig, Colo. — There is a silent architecture that holds this valley together. It is composed of water rights, power generation, and the flow of local information. For too long, we have assumed this architecture was being maintained with the same care we give our own homes.

A Technical Operational Profile conducted by Ticonderoga Systems Holdings suggests otherwise. Our plumbing — the digital and logistical connections between anchor institutions and local storefronts — is operating in a state of high transparency. This is not a failure of the people of Craig. It is a failure of the systems managed by distant interests.

"If you are a local plumber or welder, your 2027 contract is being calculated on a server in West Virginia that we just audited. They aren't protecting your future — we are."

When a zoning change is discussed in a locked PDF, our Recon engine sees the metadata. We know who is buying the land before the For Sale sign is printed. This Ledger is the only place where the public sees the same map the insiders use.
